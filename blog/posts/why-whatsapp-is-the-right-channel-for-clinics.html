Why WhatsApp is the right channel for healthcare providers

SMS is dead. Email is ignored. Apps go uninstalled within a week.

The channel your patients already open, every day, without being asked, is WhatsApp.

That single fact is enough to shape your whole engagement strategy.

WhatsApp reaches almost every smartphone in India. It works over 2G. It works when your patient is on the bus.

It supports Marathi, Hindi and English text — which covers most of the population your healthcare provider sees.

It supports voice notes for patients who can't type, and image attachments for prescriptions and reports.

It supports templates, so you can send a hundred reminders and stay compliant with policy.

And it supports two-way conversations, so patients can actually reply, ask a question, reschedule, cancel.

The wrong way to use WhatsApp is to treat it like SMS: one-way blasts, no reply expected.

That is spam, and Meta will throttle your number for it.

The right way to use WhatsApp is as a conversation channel with structured shortcuts.

Every message should end with an action the patient can take right there — confirm, reschedule, share location, view invoice.

Every campaign should be opt-in, and every opt-out should be honoured the instant it lands.

Every reminder should be timed to when the patient can act — the day before, the morning of — not at 11pm.

Every language should be the patient's language, not yours.

Every 'no' from a patient should soften the funnel, not harden it.

Get these six things right and the channel does everything you thought a patient portal was supposed to do.

It just does it inside an app the patient already understands.

That's the whole secret. There isn't a second one.

The healthcare providers that internalize it stop worrying about 'engagement' and start engaging.
