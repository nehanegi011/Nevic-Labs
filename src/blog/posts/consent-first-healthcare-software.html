Consent-first healthcare software

Consent is not a checkbox. It is a design principle that touches every screen we ship.

The patient should always know what data is being collected.

The patient should always know why it is being collected.

The patient should always be able to withdraw that consent as easily as they gave it.

The doctor should never have to think about consent in the middle of a consult.

The receptionist should never have to explain a legal disclaimer at the front desk.

That means consent has to be captured cleanly — once, in the patient's language, at the right moment.

The right moment is usually the first booking, not the tenth visit.

The right language is the patient's own, not English legalese.

The right form is a one-tap accept, not a wall of small print.

Every subsequent use of the patient's data has to trace back to that one consent.

Every screen that shows patient data has to be reachable only by people the patient would expect.

Every export, every backup, every audit log has to be tied to a purpose the patient signed up for.

Every third-party integration has to be surfaced to the patient before it runs.

Every marketing use has to be a separate, explicit opt-in — never bundled with care consent.

Every withdrawal has to propagate immediately across every downstream system.

None of this is hard, once you decide it's how you're going to build.

It is very hard to retrofit into software built without it.

That's why we build it in from day one.

A clinic that runs on consent-first software isn't just legally safer — it feels different to the patient.
