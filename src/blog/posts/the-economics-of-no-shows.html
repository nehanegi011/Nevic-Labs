The economics of no-shows

A single empty OPD slot doesn't feel like much — the doctor takes a breath, the receptionist catches up on notes.

But the math is unforgiving.

Take an OPD with 40 slots a day.

A no-show rate of 15% is 6 empty slots.

At even a modest consult fee, that's a meaningful revenue loss per day.

Compound that across a month, and it's the salary of a full-time front-desk hire.

Compound it across a year, and it's the cost of a new doctor.

That's what a 'small' no-show problem actually costs.

The fix isn't more phone calls. It's fewer, smarter reminders.

A day-before WhatsApp reminder with confirm and reschedule buttons cuts no-shows by 30–50% on its own.

A morning-of reminder catches the last few.

Together they reliably take 15% down to under 5% within four weeks.

That is real money, on the first month.

The second-order effect is even bigger: freed reception capacity to convert walk-ins.

When the phone rings less, the desk can actually greet arrivals, upsell, book follow-ups.

That's another compounding layer.

The third-order effect is patient sentiment.

A reminded patient feels cared-for, not chased.

A cared-for patient tells one more person.

Every no-show is silent revenue leaving the room. Every recovered slot pays for itself many times over.
